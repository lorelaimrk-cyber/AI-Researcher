# Distribution

Writing the article is half the work. The other half is making sure the right people read it.
Your buyers are senior people at consulting firms, and they live on LinkedIn, not on
Substack. Treat distribution as part of the product, not an afterthought.

## The email subject line

Substack sends each issue as an email. The subject line decides the open. Write it last,
after you know the sharpest claim in the piece.

Rules:
- Lead with the idea, not the topic. "The billable hour is breaking" beats "AI and
  consulting, week 1."
- Keep it under about fifty characters so it survives on a phone.
- No clickbait you cannot pay off. Curiosity that the article rewards, nothing more.
- No emoji, no all caps.

Write three candidates each week and pick the one that would make you open it.

## LinkedIn, the main channel

Every issue ships with two LinkedIn posts, produced by `prompts/05-headline-and-social.md`.

- **Post A (publish day):** the core claim of the issue as a standalone idea, three to five
  short paragraphs, ending with a soft line to the full piece. It should stand on its own
  even for someone who never clicks.
- **Post B (two days later):** a different angle on the same issue, often the risk case or one
  surprising number, so you reach the part of your audience that missed the first post.

LinkedIn rules that match your voice:
- First line is everything. It is the only thing shown before "see more." Make it a claim.
- Short lines. White space. No hashtag clutter, three at most, and only specific ones.
- Do not paste the whole article. Give one complete thought and the reason to read more.
- The link goes in the first comment if you want maximum reach, or in the post if you value
  clarity over the algorithm. Test both over a month and keep what works.

## A cadence that compounds

- **Weekly:** one issue, two LinkedIn posts.
- **Monthly:** one longer LinkedIn post that synthesizes the month's theme and links the best
  issue. This is where you reach people who do not subscribe yet.
- **Quarterly:** the predictions ledger review, posted on both Substack and LinkedIn. This is
  your trust builder and your most shareable moment.

## Slower-burn channels worth setting up once

- **A pinned LinkedIn featured section** linking your best three issues, so a new profile
  visitor sees your range immediately.
- **A one-line bio update** naming the newsletter, so every profile view is a soft pitch.
- **Cross-posting to a personal site or Medium** for search reach, with a canonical link back
  to Substack so you do not split your audience.
- **A lead magnet** once you have eight to ten issues: collect the best thinking into a short
  PDF playbook ("AI and the engineering consultancy, a field guide") and offer it for a
  subscribe. It converts profile visitors into subscribers and gives you something to point
  to in a sales conversation.

## What to measure

See [metrics-and-feedback.md](metrics-and-feedback.md). The two numbers that matter early are
open rate (is the subject working) and subscriber growth from LinkedIn (is distribution
working). Everything else is secondary until those two are healthy.
