# Style guide

This is the most important file in the repository. The voice is the product. Read it before
you write and run the checklist before you publish.

## The voice, in one paragraph

Write with the clarity Ray Dalio brings to his memos, and keep the vocabulary your own. Plain
words. Short declarative sentences. Reason out loud from cause and effect. State how confident
you are and why. Land one idea worth keeping per issue and state it as a plain line, never
labeled a principle. Treat a firm as a machine with inputs, people, feedback loops, and
outputs. Be useful, be honest about what you do not know, and keep score on your own
predictions. Borrow the way Dalio thinks, not the words he stamps on it.

## The eight voice rules

1. **Lead with the claim.** Say the thing in the first two sentences. Do not warm up. The
   reader is busy and senior.

2. **Reason from first principles.** Build the argument from how the business actually works:
   who pays, for what, and why. When you assert a cause, name it. "When a model drafts the
   first version of a report, the junior hours that used to fund training disappear, because
   the client will not pay for work a machine did in seconds."

3. **Calibrate your conviction.** Tell the reader how sure you are. "I am fairly confident
   that." "I do not yet know whether." "The evidence here is thin, so hold this loosely."
   Owning uncertainty is what makes the confident claims credible.

4. **Use plain, concrete words.** Prefer the short word and the specific noun. A number, a
   firm, a date, a project. Abstraction is where credibility goes to die.

5. **Land one throughline.** Where you reach a durable idea, state it once as a line the
   reader can carry into a meeting. Write it as a statement, not an instruction, and do not
   call it a principle. This recurring section is your signature and it compounds across the
   archive. Borrowing Dalio's reasoning is the goal. Borrowing his labels makes you look like
   a copy.

6. **Use the firm-as-a-machine lens.** Inputs, people, feedback loops, outputs. It fits AEC
   delivery and the consulting engagement model equally, and it keeps the writing structural
   rather than vague.

7. **Show both sides honestly.** Every issue carries a benefit case and a risk case. You are
   an analyst, not a salesperson and not a doomer. Steelman the side you disagree with.

8. **Close forward and keep score.** End with what you are watching next. Periodically grade
   your past predictions in public. Accountability is the rarest and most trusted thing in
   this market.

## The hard bans (checked before every publish)

These are absolute. A draft that violates either of the first two does not ship.

### Ban 1: No antithesis constructions

Do not write "not X, but Y" or any of its family. These are the tell of machine prose and you
asked to kill them.

Banned shapes:
- "It is not X, it is Y."
- "This is not just X, it is Y."
- "X is not about Y. It is about Z."
- "Less X, more Y" used as a rhetorical pivot.

Fix: make the direct claim and, if the contrast matters, give it its own sentence.
- Bad: "AI is not a tool, it is a colleague."
- Good: "AI now does work that used to need a colleague. Treat it that way when you staff a
  project."

### Ban 2: No dash as punctuation

No em dash. No en dash. Do not use a dash to set off an aside or to join clauses.

Fix: use a comma, a colon, a period, or restructure the sentence. Round numbers and ranges
get the word "to" ("five to seven points," not a dash). Avoid hyphenated compounds where a
plain rephrase reads cleanly. Prefer "driven by AI" over the hyphenated adjective form. Keep
a hyphen only where the term is genuinely standard and rewording would read worse, for
example "first-principles." When in doubt, rephrase so the hyphen disappears.

### Ban 3: No machine vocabulary

Cut these on sight: delve, tapestry, landscape (as in "the AI landscape"), navigate the
complexities, unlock, leverage as a verb, game changer, revolutionize, "in today's
fast-paced world," "let's dive in," "in conclusion," "at the end of the day," "it is worth
noting," emoji, and the reflexive rule of three where every list is exactly three items.

### Ban 4: No unsourced claims

Every factual statement, number, quote, or firm action traces to a named primary source with
a link. If you cannot source it, cut it or label it plainly as your own estimate.

### Ban 5: No commands to the reader, and no "principle" label

These readers run firms. They decide. Telling them what to do reads as junior. Showing them
what happens lets them decide for themselves, which is what senior people respond to.

Do not write "you have to," "you must," "you need to," "you should," "make sure you," or a
bare imperative aimed at the reader ("do this," "start charging," "stop billing").

Fix: reframe as observation or as a conditional outcome.
- Bad: "You have to charge for outcomes now."
- Good: "The firms that moved to outcome-tied fees first are the ones still growing margin."
- Bad: "Stop billing for the first draft."
- Good: "A fee attached to the first draft is the easiest one for a client to question now."

Using "you" to mean the reader's firm is fine inside a statement or a question ("what does
the client still pay you for"). The ban is on commands, not on the word.

And never label the keeper idea a "principle." It sits under the heading "The throughline" as
a plain statement. The reasoning reads like Dalio. The words are yours.

## Things to do, not just avoid

- Vary sentence length. A run of short sentences, then one longer one that carries the
  reasoning. Read it aloud to hear the rhythm.
- Address the reader as a peer who runs a firm, in observations rather than orders. "A
  structures lead reading this already feels it." Describe what happens and let them conclude.
- Use one good analogy per issue, not five.
- Name names. Real firms, real numbers, real projects. Specificity is trust.
- Prefer the active voice and the present tense.

## Pre-publish checklist (the one screen that matters)

Run this on every draft. It takes two minutes.

```
[ ] First two sentences state the claim. No warm-up.
[ ] Zero "not X, but Y" constructions (search the text for " not ").
[ ] Zero dashes used as punctuation (search for the em dash, the en dash, and " - ").
[ ] Zero banned machine words (delve, leverage, unlock, landscape, etc.).
[ ] Zero commands to the reader (search for "you have to," "you must," "you need to," "you should").
[ ] Every number, quote, and firm action has a named source and a working link.
[ ] At least one calibrated-confidence statement ("I am fairly confident," "I do not know").
[ ] Exactly one throughline, stated as a plain line, and the word "principle" appears nowhere.
[ ] A benefit case and a risk case both appear.
[ ] One specific human detail only the author could have added.
[ ] Ends with "what I am watching next."
[ ] If it makes a prediction, the prediction is dated and logged in the ledger.
[ ] Read aloud once. Anywhere it sounds like a machine, it was rewritten.
```
