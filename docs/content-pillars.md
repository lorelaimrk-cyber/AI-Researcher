# Content pillars

Five recurring themes. Every issue sits mainly in one pillar. Over a quarter you cover all
five, so the archive reads as a coherent body of thought instead of a pile of news reactions.
`scripts/gather.py` groups the weekly brief by these pillars, so the mapping is mechanical.

## Pillar 1: The economics of billable expertise

The flagship pillar and the home of the core thesis. What AI does to the billable hour, the
staffing pyramid, utilization, pricing, and how firms win and scope work. Fixed-price versus
time and materials. The shrinking value of junior hours and the rising value of judgment,
relationships, and accountability.

Typical questions: If a model drafts the report, what does the client still pay for? What
happens to margin when the work that filled timesheets takes minutes? How do you price a
deliverable a machine helped make?

## Pillar 2: Delivery and tooling

How the work actually gets done under AI. Design automation, generative design, BIM and
digital twins, simulation, code and spec generation, document review, agents inside the
delivery workflow. What is real in production versus what is a demo.

Typical questions: Which tools are firms actually deploying on live projects? Where do they
break? What is the gap between the vendor pitch and the site reality?

## Pillar 3: Risk, liability, and governance

Who is responsible when a model is in the loop. Professional liability, the engineer of
record, code compliance, insurance, data security, client confidentiality, model error and
hallucination on technical work, and the emerging rules. This pillar is where the AEC reader
and the management consulting reader feel the most exposure.

Typical questions: Who signs and who is liable when AI produced the calculation? What does a
regulator or an insurer now expect? How do you govern model use without killing the upside?

## Pillar 4: Talent and organization design

What AI does to how firms hire, train, and structure themselves. The training pipeline
problem (juniors learned by doing the work AI now does), the changing shape of teams, new
roles, skills that gain and lose value, and what it means to be a young engineer when the
first rungs of the ladder are automated.

Typical questions: If juniors no longer cut their teeth on grunt work, how does anyone become
senior? What roles appear and disappear? How do you keep the people who hold the judgment?

## Pillar 5: Clients and the market

The demand side and the competitive board. What clients now expect, how AI changes what they
will pay for, new entrants and AI native competitors, mergers and acquisitions, big firm
strategy moves, and how the work itself is being redefined by buyers who also have AI.

Typical questions: What do clients now do in house that they used to buy? Who is entering the
market because AI lowered the cost to compete? How are the largest firms positioning?

## How to use the pillars

- Each issue is tagged with one primary pillar. A secondary pillar is fine.
- Rotate. Do not run the economics pillar four weeks straight even though it is the flagship.
- Track which pillars pull the most opens and shares in
  [metrics-and-feedback.md](metrics-and-feedback.md), then reweight the calendar toward what
  resonates, while still covering all five over a quarter.
