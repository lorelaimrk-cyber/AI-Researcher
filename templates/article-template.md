## The claim

State the argument in the first two sentences. No warm-up. The reader should know exactly what
you think and why it matters before the end of the first paragraph.

## What happened

The news that makes this current, in plain terms, with the key facts and numbers. Every fact
carries a named source and a link. Two or three short paragraphs, not a news dump. The reader
wants the part that matters, not the press release.

## Why it matters to a firm like yours

Connect the news to how the reader's firm makes money, delivers work, carries risk, hires, or
competes. Use the firm-as-a-machine lens. This is where you reason from first principles and
where the value sits. Address the reader as a peer who runs a firm.

## The benefit case

The honest upside. What a firm gains if it moves well. Be concrete about who gains and how.

## The risk case

The honest downside. What breaks, who is exposed, what gets worse if a firm moves badly or too
fast. Steelman the side you disagree with. Calibrate your confidence here at least once.
