# The billable hour is repricing, and the firms know it

How the people who sell engineering judgment are changing what they charge for, before the market changes it for them.

## The claim

The price of an hour of expert work is falling, and the largest consulting firms are now saying so out loud. I am fairly confident that within a few years the firms that still bill the old way, by the hour for work a machine can draft in seconds, will lose the best work to the firms that learned to charge for something else. The question worth sitting with this week is simple. When the model does the first draft, what does the client still pay for?

## What happened

This week the chief executive of Boston Consulting Group, Christoph Schweizer, told investors how AI is changing the way his firm gets paid. On most of BCG's largest AI projects, the fee now moves with the result the client gets, such as a lower cost base or higher revenue. Roughly three quarters of those largest cases carry a variable fee of this kind, though fees tied to outcomes are still less than a third of the firm's work overall. BCG expects AI work to grow from about a fifth of revenue in 2024 to about two fifths by 2026, and the firm's revenue rose seven percent to 14.4 billion dollars in its latest year while headcount kept growing. The reporting was carried by Business Insider ([via AOL](https://www.aol.com/finance/ai-forcing-mckinsey-bcg-bain-063300951.html)) and summarized by [BCG itself](https://www.bcg.com/about/expertise/ai-changing-consulting-and-pricing-models).

BCG is not alone. About a quarter of McKinsey's global fees are now tied to outcomes rather than hours, and its UK managing partner Michael Birshan put it plainly: "We're doing more performance based arrangements with our clients." McKinsey's internal assistant, Lilli, runs more than 500,000 prompts a month, and consultants report time savings of up to thirty percent. Bain says roughly thirty percent of its consulting business is now enabled by AI and expects that to reach half ([AOL](https://www.aol.com/finance/ai-forcing-mckinsey-bcg-bain-063300951.html)).

Then there is the sharper signal. West Monroe, a midsize advisory firm, has put six AI agents online for free at WestMonroe.ai. You type a company name and receive a report of five to ten pages on questions like competitive strategy and business model risk. Its chief AI officer, Bret Greenstein, calls this "phase zero" of consulting, and he is blunt about the economics. The early analysis that "once would have cost clients hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars" now has a marginal cost close to zero, so "to lock them behind some sort of list of people or fees doesn't make a lot of sense" ([AOL](https://www.aol.com/articles/consulting-firms-ai-chief-explains-130358000.html)).

## Why it matters to a firm like yours

A firm works like a machine. The inputs are people and time. The output is judgment a client will pay for and stand behind. For decades the machine ran on a pyramid: a wide base of junior staff doing the searching, the drafting, the modeling, and the document review, with senior people on top shaping the answer and carrying the accountability. The billable hour was the meter on this machine, and it worked because the junior hours were genuinely scarce and genuinely useful.

AI attacks the base of that pyramid directly. The first draft of a report, the first pass over a set of drawings, the first model of a structure, the first read of a contract, these are exactly the tasks where a capable model is fastest. In the AEC world the same week brought the same signal from a different angle. Bluebeam acquired mbue, a startup whose software uses AI to review construction drawings and specifications, and folded it in as a "talent and technology" investment ([AEC Magazine](https://aecmag.com/construction/bluebeam-acquires-mbue-in-talent-and-technology-investment/)). The contractor McCarthy partnered with Palantir to push AI into its operations ([Construction Dive](https://www.constructiondive.com/news/mccarthy-palantir-artificial-intelligence-ai-partnership/822517/)). The work that used to fill a junior engineer's timesheet is becoming the work a tool does before lunch.

When the cost of producing the first layer of analysis falls close to zero, the price a client will pay for that layer falls with it. That is not a threat from some distant future. The biggest firms in the world are already repricing around it, in public, this quarter.

## The benefit case

The upside is real and I do not want to undersell it. A firm that moves well gets to sell results instead of effort. When you charge for the outcome rather than the hour, a faster machine makes you more profitable, not less, because the value you deliver is no longer capped by how many people you can put on a plane. BCG growing revenue and headcount at the same time as it shifts to variable fees is evidence that the pie can grow ([AOL](https://www.aol.com/finance/ai-forcing-mckinsey-bcg-bain-063300951.html)). The firms describe a near limitless demand for help putting AI to work. The work changes, and there is more of it, [YOUR DETAIL HERE].

## The risk case

The risk is structural and it is the part I would lose sleep over. The pyramid was never only a billing model. It was the training system. People became senior by doing the junior work, by making small mistakes on small things and learning judgment in their hands. If a model now does the work that taught the next generation, you can win this year's margin and quietly break your own supply of senior judgment ten years out. I hold this view with medium confidence, because firms could build new ways to train people, but I see far more energy going into deploying the tools than into rebuilding the apprenticeship the tools dissolve.

There is a second risk, and West Monroe is the warning. If one credible firm gives away the first layer of insight for free, it resets what every client expects to pay for that layer everywhere. Greenstein is steelmanning his own move when he says judgment and accountability "are uniquely ours," and he may be right ([AOL](https://www.aol.com/articles/consulting-firms-ai-chief-explains-130358000.html)). The danger is that the free layer keeps rising. What is phase zero this year becomes phase one next year, and the floor under your fees keeps moving up the value chain toward the senior judgment you assumed was safe.

## The throughline

> The fee that survives is the one attached to judgment and accountability, the two parts of the work a model cannot sign its name to. Any fee attached to work a machine can now do is one the market is about to take back.

## A prediction

> Prediction (2026-06-13): By the end of 2027, at least one of McKinsey, BCG, or Bain will report publicly that more than half of its fees are tied to outcomes rather than hours. Confidence: medium.

I am logging this in the ledger so you can hold me to it. Today McKinsey sits near a quarter and BCG keeps its outcome work under a third overall, so this is a real call, not a safe one.

## What I am watching next

Two signals. First, whether any firm starts reporting how it trains juniors now that the juniors do less of the old junior work. The firm that solves the apprenticeship problem under AI will own the next decade of talent, and almost no one is talking about it yet. Second, whether the free tier keeps climbing. If a second large firm follows West Monroe and gives away something a tier more valuable than a five page strategy sketch, the repricing is moving faster than the firms are admitting.
